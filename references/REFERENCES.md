# References — Marathon Drink Calculator

Every claim on the calculator page traces to one of the citations below. PDFs
can be downloaded by running `bash fetch-references.sh` from this directory
on a machine with normal outbound network access.

## Full citations

### Carbohydrate intake & oxidation

1. **Burke LM, Jeukendrup AE, Jones AM, Mooses M.** Contemporary Nutrition
   Strategies to Optimize Performance in Distance Runners and Race Walkers.
   *Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab* 2019;29(2):117–129.
   PMID 30943841. <https://journals.humankinetics.com/view/journals/ijsnem/29/2/article-p117.xml>
   - Marathon row (events > 2.5 h): **60–90 g/h CHO** for trained runners.

2. **Hearris MA et al.** Multiple transportable carbohydrates ingested at
   120 g·h⁻¹: drink, gel, chew, and mix formats.
   *J Appl Physiol* 2022;132(6):1394–1406.
   <https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/japplphysiol.00091.2022>
   - Peak exogenous CHO oxidation 1.56–1.66 g/min at 120 g/h ingestion, 0.8:1 fructose:maltodextrin.

3. **Jentjens RL, Jeukendrup AE.** High rates of exogenous carbohydrate
   oxidation from a mixture of glucose and fructose ingested during prolonged
   cycling. *Br J Nutr* 2005;93(4):485–492.
   <https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/5978786559A73EDEFC6DCD272A773E3A>
   - 1.75 ± 0.11 g/min (GLU+FRU) vs 1.26 ± 0.07 g/min (GLU alone).

4. **Trommelen J et al.** Fructose and Sucrose Intake Increase Exogenous CHO
   Oxidation during Exercise. *Nutrients* 2017;9(2):167. PMC 5331598.
   <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5331598/>
   - At 1.8 g/min ingestion: 0.96 (GLU) vs 1.40 (GLU+FRU) vs 1.29 (GLU+SUC) g/min oxidation.

5. **Jentjens RL, Achten J, Jeukendrup AE.** High oxidation rates from
   combined carbohydrates ingested during exercise.
   *Metabolism* 2005;54(5):610–618. PMID 15877301.
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15877301/>
   - Sucrose alone at 1.2 g/min → 0.98 g/min exogenous oxidation.

6. **Podlogar T, Wallis GA.** New Horizons in Carbohydrate Research and
   Application for Endurance Athletes. *Sports Med* 2022;52(Suppl 1):5–23.
   PMC 9734239. <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9734239/>

7. **Viribay A et al.** Effects of 120 g/h CHO intake during a marathon on
   running performance and muscle damage. *Nutrients* 2020;12(5):1367.
   PMC 7284742. <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7284742/>
   - 120 g/h reduced CK/LDH/GOT vs 60 and 90 g/h in elite mountain marathoners.

8. **Podlogar T et al.** Exogenous CHO oxidation at 90 vs 120 g·h⁻¹.
   *Eur J Appl Physiol* 2022;122(11):2393–2401. PMC 9560939.
   <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9560939/>

### Gastric emptying & gut training

9. **Murray R et al.** The effects of glucose, fructose, and sucrose ingestion
   during exercise. *Int J Sport Nutr* 1999;9(3):263–274. PMID 10477362.
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10477362/>
   - 8 % CHO drink significantly slows gastric emptying vs water/4 %/6 %.

10. **Jeukendrup AE.** Training the Gut for Athletes.
    *Sports Med* 2017;47(Suppl 1):101–110. PMC 5371619.
    <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5371619/>
    - SGLT1 and GLUT5 upregulate with repeated CHO exposure during exercise.

### Hydration & sodium

11. **Lara B et al.** Interindividual variability in sweat electrolyte
    concentration in marathoners. *Eur J Appl Physiol* 2016. PMC 4966593.
    <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4966593/>
    - 157 marathoners; sweat [Na+] 42.9 ± 18.7 mmol/L (range 7–96).

12. **Sawka MN, Cheuvront SN, Kenefick RW.** ACSM Position Stand: Exercise and
    Fluid Replacement. *Med Sci Sports Exerc* 2007 (and 2015 updates).
    PMID 17277604. <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17277604/>
    - 0.5–2.5 L/h marathon sweat-rate range; basis for the 6–12 ml/kg/h heuristic.

13. **Hoffman MD, Stuempfle KJ.** Sodium intake during an ultramarathon does
    not prevent muscle cramping, dehydration, or hyponatremia.
    *Sports Med* 2015. PMC 4688305.
    <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4688305/>
    - Overdrinking plain water is the dominant exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) risk.

### Caffeine & bicarbonate

14. **Burke LM.** Caffeine and sports performance.
    *Appl Physiol Nutr Metab* 2008;33(6):1319–1334. PMID 19088794.
    <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19088794/>
    - 3–6 mg/kg ~60 min pre-exercise; no extra benefit above 9 mg/kg.

15. **Grgic J et al.** International Society of Sports Nutrition position
    stand: sodium bicarbonate and exercise performance.
    *J Int Soc Sports Nutr* 2021;18:61. PMC 8427947.
    <https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8427947/>
    - Optimal dose 0.2–0.3 g/kg, 60–180 min pre, split-dose with carb-rich food.
    - Strongest evidence for 30 s – 12 min high-intensity events; marathon-specific evidence is mixed.

### Commercial reference

16. **Maurten Drink Mix 320** — product page.
    <https://www.maurten.com/products/drink-mix-320-box-us>
    - 80 g carbs per 500 ml = 16 % w/v.
    - Ingredients: maltodextrin, fructose, sodium alginate, pectin, salt, sodium bicarbonate.
    - Hydrogel forms in stomach acid, masking the osmolality of a 16 % drink.

## Calculator constants — derivation

- **1 g NaCl = 0.393 g sodium** (Na 22.99 / NaCl 58.44). Calculator rounds to
  0.4 g for arithmetic convenience; +1.7 % over-estimate has no practical
  effect at race-day doses.
- **1 g sucrose ≈ 0.5 g glucose + 0.5 g fructose** after hydrolysis in the
  gut. Strictly each gram yields ~0.526 g of each monosaccharide once water
  is added (sucrose 342 g/mol + 18 g/mol H₂O → glucose 180 + fructose 180),
  but for sports-nutrition labelling 1 g sucrose = 1 g carbs.
- **Fluid ml/kg/h heuristic** (6 cool / 8 mild / 12 hot): centred on
  marathoner sweat-rate distributions from Sawka 2007 ACSM and Beis et al.
  2012. Hot-day rates can reach 14 ml/kg/h in salty sweaters running above
  25 °C — use the salty-sweater radio plus your training data if so.
- **Sodium 300–1000 mg/h range**: matches ACSM 2007 baseline (300–600 mg/h)
  extended to 800–1000 mg/h for heat / salty sweaters per modern marathon
  guidance (Featherstone, ISSN).

## Notes

- Several papers are paywalled (Burke 2008, Murray 1999, Jentjens Metabolism
  2005, Sawka 2007). The `fetch-references.sh` script lists them as such
  and skips them. PubMed abstracts cover the cited specific numbers.
- The build sandbox blocks outbound traffic to PMC / journal hosts; PDFs are
  not committed to the repo. Run the fetch script on your own machine.
