02 / FUEL FORMATS

Beyond the
bottle.

DIY gels, real food, and what can go wrong

Your drink covers carbs, water and salt all at once. But there's a limit to how much sugar you can dissolve in it before it stops emptying from your stomach. Past that point you need gels or real food on the side. Pfeiffer (2010) showed that gels and drinks get absorbed at the same rate when the dose is matched. The form mostly affects taste and how your stomach handles it, not what actually gets in.

DIY gel recipesAbout 60 % carbs. Made from stuff you have at home.

Aim for 20 to 25 g of carbs in a 30 to 40 g serving. Each recipe below makes 3 to 5 servings. Squeeze into a small soft flask or the corner of a zip-lock bag. Honey and maple gels keep at room temperature. Date and rice-cake versions need the fridge until race morning.

Allen Lim rice cakesFrom Feed Zone Portables (Skratch Labs, 2013)

A classic for slower endurance fueling. It's cooked sushi rice held together with egg, lightly sweetened and salted.

  • 1½ cups (about 300 g cooked) short-grain white rice
  • 2 eggs, lightly beaten
  • 50 g cane sugar, ½ tsp sea salt, a pinch of cinnamon
  • Cook the eggs and sugar in a pan until they're a soft scramble. Fold in the warm rice. Press it into a parchment-lined dish. Chill for 30 minutes. Cut into 50 g squares. Wrap each one in foil.

Each square is about 25 g of carbs and 100 mg of sodium. It will survive in your pocket on a long run for 2 to 3 hours.

How to use themOn race day

  • Stack, don't swap. Use gels and snacks on top of your drink, not instead of it. The drink gives you your water and salt. Gels add extra carbs.
  • One gel is about 25 g of carbs. Take one every 25 to 45 minutes depending on your hourly target.
  • Always chase with water. Gels are about 60 % carbs. Without 100 to 150 ml of plain water after, the gel just sits in your stomach.
  • Match what you eat to how fast you're running. Faster than about 7 min/mile, stick to liquids and gels. Slower than about 8 min/mile, real food is fine.
  • Practice it at least 4 times. The SDA/USSF 2025 position statement says the biggest things you can do to avoid GI problems are training your gut, eating enough carbs and protein, staying hydrated, and watching FODMAPs. Of those, practicing your exact fueling plan is the one most in your control.

What can go wrongRead this before race week

Fructose

Too much fructose. Your drink is already about half fructose (that's what sucrose breaks down into). Add a honey or date gel, also half fructose, and you can push past the gut's fructose limit of about 60 g per hour. Past that you get bloating and a sudden need for a toilet. If you're going above 75 g of carbs per hour, get the extra carbs from rice syrup or maltodextrin instead.

Water

Gels without water. A gel is about three times more concentrated than your drink. Without 100 to 150 ml of water right after, it just sits in your stomach. Take gels at an aid station so the water is right there.

Caffeine

Caffeine creeps up on you. Many shop-bought gels have 25 to 100 mg of caffeine each. Stack those with instant coffee in your bottles and a pre-race espresso, and you can be at 6 mg per kg of body weight by halfway. Anything above about 9 mg/kg gives no extra benefit (Burke 2008). Add it up.

Fiber and fat

Hidden fiber and fat in "natural" snacks. Whole-grain bread, nut butter, granola bars look healthy, but the fiber and fat slow your stomach down. For a marathon, stick with white rice and white bread. Skip the nut butter on race day.

Hydrogel

Hydrogel is mostly marketing. Two independent trials (McCubbin 2020; Li 2026) found no difference in how much glucose you absorb, how much you burn, GI symptoms, or performance compared to drinks of the same strength without the hydrogel. What actually makes Maurten work is its mix of maltodextrin and fructose at 16 % concentration, not the gel itself. You can get the same effect with sucrose at 8 to 10 % plus a water chase.

Practice

Test it in training, not on race day. Try each gel recipe and snack at your goal marathon pace at least 3 or 4 times during weeks 5 to 8 of gut training.

03 / GUT TRAINING

Six to ten
weeks out.

On long runs, at race pace, building up slowly

Your gut has two transporters that pull carbs out of the intestine. One (SGLT1) handles glucose. The other (GLUT5) handles fructose. Both get better at the job the more you ask them to do it during exercise. Start six to ten weeks before race day and slowly add more carbs to your long runs at goal pace.

Weeks 1 and 230 g/h6 %Drink on a schedule, every 15 to 20 minutes. Get used to swallowing while breathing hard.

Rules

The basic rules. Train your gut on hard efforts, not just easy runs. Eat your race-day breakfast before any gut-training session. Don't try a new fuel in the last two weeks before race day.

If it hurts

If your gut acts up (bloating, cramping, nausea, sudden diarrhea), drop back one phase, stay there for two weeks, then try moving up again. Don't push through. More concentrated carbs hitting a stomach that's not emptying just makes it worse.

Race week

Race week. Three days out, cut the fiber. No whole grains, salads, bran, or beans. Aim for 8 to 10 g of carbs per kg of body weight per day. The day before, keep it low in fat. Mostly white rice, pasta, bread, or potato.

04 / BICARBONATE

Optional
buffer.

Worth a try, or fine to skip

Plain baking soda soaks up acid in your blood and can give you a small bump in performance, maybe a few percent. It works best at harder efforts like 5k or 10k pace. For a marathon the effect is smaller and varies a lot from person to person. Only worth trying if you'll be running near 80 % of your V̇O₂max and you've practiced it.

Dose and timingFor a 70 kg runner

ProtocolDoseFor youWhen
Conservative 0.2 g/kg 14 g, about 2¾ tsp Split it in two. Half at 180 min before the start, half at 90 min before. Both with a carb-rich meal.
Standard 0.3 g/kg 21 g, about 4 tsp Split it into 3 or 4 portions, every 30 to 40 min, finishing 60 to 90 min before the start. Each one with food.

One level teaspoon of baking soda is about 5 to 6 g. Dissolve it in 250 ml of water. Chase each dose with another 250 ml of plain water and some food (a bagel, a banana, oats).

Stomach

The stomach risk is real. Common side effects: bloating, nausea, diarrhea, vomiting. To soften that: split the dose, take it with carb-rich food, drink plenty of water, and start at 0.2 g/kg the first 2 or 3 times. Never try bicarb for the first time on race day. Practice it 3 to 5 times on long runs first.

Skip if

Skip bicarb if you have high blood pressure, kidney problems, or a history of bicarb making you sick even after practice. Baking soda is 27 % sodium by weight. A standard 0.3 g/kg dose for a 70 kg runner is about 5.7 g of sodium on top of what you're already drinking. Don't double up.

How

How to take it. Mix the dose into water. It fizzes and tastes salty. A bit of lemon juice helps. Drink it with your pre-race meal. The buffering effect peaks 90 to 180 minutes after your last dose, which is why splitting it across the 60 to 180 minutes before the start works.

For a marathon

Worth it for a marathon? The evidence for bicarb is strongest for 1 to 10 minute all-out efforts. For a marathon, expect a smaller, more individual effect. If your goal pace is one you could hold a conversation at, the stomach risk probably outweighs the benefit. Skip it.
