Getting your gut ready

The intestinal carb transporters (SGLT1 for glucose, GLUT5 for fructose) upregulate with repeated exposure during exercise. Start 6–10 weeks before race day and ramp on your long runs at goal pace.

PhaseLong-run carb targetDrink %What to practice
Weeks 1–230 g/h6 %Drink on time (every 15–20 min). Get used to swallowing while breathing hard.
Weeks 3–445–60 g/h6–8 %Add salt to taste. Practice grabbing the bottle without breaking pace.
Weeks 5–660–75 g/h8 %One long run at marathon pace with race-day breakfast.
Weeks 7–875–90 g/h8–10 %Full dress rehearsal: bottle, salt, coffee timing, exact recipe.
Weeks 9+ (advanced)90–100 g/h10–12 %Concentrated flask + plain-water chase. Only if you tolerated week 7–8 cleanly.
Key rules. Train the gut at race intensity, not just easy runs. Always pair gut-training sessions with the planned race-day breakfast. Never introduce a new fuel in the last 2 weeks before race day.
If you get GI distress (bloating, cramping, nausea, urgent diarrhea): drop one phase back, hold for another 2 weeks, then try again. Don't push through — concentrated carbs hitting a non-emptying stomach makes it worse.
Race-week tapering. 3 days out: low-fiber meals (no whole grains, no salads, no bran, no beans). Carbohydrate-load to ~8–10 g/kg/day. The day before: low-fat, mostly white rice/pasta/bread/potato.

Sodium bicarbonate (optional)

Plain baking soda (NaHCO3) buffers blood acidity and can squeeze out a few percent of performance — strongest evidence is at higher relative intensities (5k–10k pace). For a marathon, benefit is smaller and more individual; only worth it if you'll be running near 80 % V̇O2max and have practiced it.

Dose & timing for your body mass

ProtocolSodium bicarb doseFor 70 kgWhen
Conservative (lower GI risk) 0.2 g/kg 14 g (~2¾ tsp) Split-dose: half 180 min pre-start, half 90 min pre-start, both with a carb-rich meal.
Standard (most studied) 0.3 g/kg 21 g (~4 tsp) Split-dose: 3–4 portions every 30–40 min, finishing 60–90 min before start, each with food.

1 level teaspoon of baking soda ≈ 5–6 g. Dissolve in ~250 ml water; chase each dose with another ~250 ml plain water and food (a bagel, banana, oats).

GI risk is real. Common: bloating, nausea, diarrhea, vomiting. Mitigate by (1) split-dosing, (2) taking with carb-rich food, (3) drinking plenty of water, (4) starting at 0.2 g/kg the first 2–3 times you trial it. Never race-day-debut bicarb. Practice it 3–5 times on long runs first.
Skip bicarb if you have high blood pressure, kidney issues, or a history of bicarb-induced GI distress that didn't resolve with practice. The extra sodium (about 1100 mg sodium per 3 g of baking soda) also stacks on top of your in-race sodium plan — don't double up.
How to take it. Mix the dose into a glass of water (it fizzes/tastes salty — lemon juice helps). Drink with your pre-race meal. The buffering peak is roughly 90–180 min after the last dose, which is why split-loading 60–180 min pre-start works.
Marathon context. Bicarb's evidence base is strongest for 1–10 min all-out efforts; for the marathon, expect a smaller and more individual benefit. If your goal pace is conversational, the GI risk likely outweighs the gain — skip it.

Pantry chemistry

  • Sucrose (cane/beet sugar) → gut hydrolysis = 50 % glucose + 50 % fructose. Natural 1:1 ratio.
  • Dextrose = pure glucose. Use to tilt ratio toward glucose at >60 g/h.
  • Fructose powder = needed only if chasing Maurten’s 1:0.8 fructose:glucose at 100+ g/h.
  • Salt co-transports glucose via SGLT1; also primary electrolyte.
  • Citric acid / lemon juice = flavor and palatability.

Concentration zones

  • ≤ 8 %: fast gastric emptying, isotonic, single-bottle strategy works.
  • 8–10 %: still tolerable; gut-train before relying on it.
  • 10–14 %: Maurten Drink Mix 320 territory (uses hydrogel). At home: drink plain water alongside.
  • 14–18 %: concentrated flask strategy — sip + chase with aid-station water.
  • > 18 %: not recommended; GI distress likely.

Gut-training ramp (4–10 weeks)

  • Wk 1–2: 30 g/h, 6 % drink
  • Wk 3–4: 45–60 g/h, 6–8 %
  • Wk 5–6: 60–75 g/h, 8 %
  • Wk 7–8: 75–90 g/h, 8–10 %
  • Wk 9+ (elite only): 90–120 g/h, 12–18 % concentrated flask + water

Train the gut at race intensity and race pace. Rehearse the exact recipe 4–6 times before race day. Never introduce new fuel in the last 2 weeks.

Safety

  • Hyponatremia red flags: headache, confusion, swelling, weight gain mid-race — stop plain water, eat salt.
  • GI distress: stop concentrated carbs, switch to small sips of plain water, walk if needed.
  • Pre-race meal: low-fiber, low-fat, carb-dominant. No new foods.
  • Mix the drink fully — undissolved sugar at the bottom skews the last sips.
