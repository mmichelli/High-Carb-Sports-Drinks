02 / FUEL FORMATS

Beyond the
bottle.

DIY gels · real food · risks

Drinks deliver carbs, fluid and sodium together — but the stomach has limits. When you need more grams than your bottle can carry without diluting hydration, gels and real food step in. Pfeiffer 2010 showed gels match drinks on oxidation at matched intake: form mostly affects palatability and GI tolerance, not absorption.

DIY gel recipes~60 % carb · pantry only

Target ~20–25 g carbs per ~30–40 g serving. Each recipe below makes 3–5 servings. Squeeze into a small soft flask or zip-lock corner. Honey- and maple-based gels are shelf-stable; date and rice-cake versions need refrigeration until race morning.

Allen Lim rice cakesSkratch Labs · Feed Zone Portables 2013

The gold standard for slower-paced endurance fueling — cooked sushi rice held together with egg, sweetened lightly, salted.

  • 1½ cups (~300 g cooked) short-grain white rice
  • 2 eggs, lightly beaten
  • 50 g cane sugar · ½ tsp sea salt · pinch cinnamon
  • Cook eggs & sugar in a pan until soft scramble; fold in warm rice; press into a parchment-lined dish; chill 30 min; cut into 50 g squares; wrap in foil.

Each square ≈ 25 g carbs + ~100 mg sodium. Survives a long-run pocket for 2–3 hours.

How to use themRace-day execution

  • Stack, don't swap. Use gels and snacks on top of your drink, not instead. Drink = fluid & sodium base; gels = carb top-up.
  • One gel ≈ 25 g carbs, every 25–45 min depending on hourly target.
  • Always chase with water. Gels are ~60 % carb concentration — without 100–150 ml plain-water chase, gastric emptying stalls.
  • Match form to pace. Faster than ~7 min/mi: liquids & gels only. Slower than ~8 min/mi: real food is viable.
  • Rehearse ≥ 4×. Costa & Gaskell 2025 put gut-training and tested-fueling as the two most-evidenced GI mitigators.

Risks & common mistakesRead before race week

Fructose

Fructose stacking. Your drink already has ~50 % fructose (from sucrose). Add a honey or date gel (also ~50 % fructose) and you can blow past the GLUT5 ceiling (~60 g/h fructose) — bloating, urgent diarrhea. Above 75 g/h total carbs, lean on rice-syrup or maltodextrin-based fuels for the extra grams.

Water chase

Concentrated carbs without water. A gel is ~3× more concentrated than your drink. Without a 100–150 ml water chase, gastric emptying stalls. Always take the gel within one minute of an aid station.

Caffeine

Caffeine accumulation. Many commercial gels carry 25–100 mg caffeine each. Stacked with in-bottle instant coffee + a pre-race espresso, you can hit 6 mg/kg by halfway. Above ~9 mg/kg gives no further benefit (Burke 2008). Track your total.

Fiber & fat

Hidden fiber + fat in "natural" snacks. Whole-grain bread, nut butter, granola bars — popular pantry picks, but the fiber + fat slows emptying. For a marathon, prefer refined-grain (white rice, white bread) and skip nut butters race-day.

Hydrogel

Hydrogel is mostly marketing. Two independent RCTs (McCubbin 2020; Li 2026 review) found no improvement in glucose availability, CHO oxidation, GI symptoms, or performance vs concentration-matched controls. Maurten's edge is its composition (1:0.8 malto:fru at 16 %), not the alginate matrix. Reproducible with sucrose at 8–10 % + a water chase.

Rehearse

Test in training, not on race day. Rehearse each gel recipe and snack at goal marathon pace at least 3–4 times during gut-training weeks 5–8.

03 / GUT TRAINING

Six to ten
weeks out.

Long runs · race pace · ramp

The intestinal carbohydrate transporters — SGLT1 for glucose, GLUT5 for fructose — upregulate with repeated exposure during exercise. Start six to ten weeks before race day and ramp on long runs at goal pace.

PhaseLong-run carb targetDrink %What to practice
Weeks 1–230 g/h6 %Drink on time (every 15–20 min). Get used to swallowing while breathing hard.
Weeks 3–445–60 g/h6–8 %Add salt to taste. Practice grabbing the bottle without breaking pace.
Weeks 5–660–75 g/h8 %One long run at marathon pace with race-day breakfast.
Weeks 7–875–90 g/h8–10 %Full dress rehearsal: bottle, salt, coffee timing, exact recipe.
Weeks 9+90–100 g/h10–12 %Concentrated flask + plain-water chase. Only if you tolerated weeks 7–8 cleanly.
Rules

Key rules. Train the gut at race intensity, not just easy runs. Always pair gut-training sessions with the planned race-day breakfast. Never introduce a new fuel in the last two weeks before race day.

GI distress

If GI distress appears — bloating, cramping, nausea, urgent diarrhea — drop one phase back, hold for two weeks, then try again. Don't push through; concentrated carbs hitting a non-emptying stomach makes it worse.

Taper

Race-week tapering. Three days out: low-fiber meals (no whole grains, salads, bran, beans). Carb-load to 8–10 g/kg/day. Day before: low-fat, mostly white rice / pasta / bread / potato.

04 / BICARBONATE

Optional
buffer.

Practice or skip

Plain baking soda buffers blood acidity and can squeeze a few percent of performance — strongest at higher relative intensities (5k–10k pace). For a marathon, the benefit is smaller and more individual; only worth it if you'll be running near 80 % V̇O₂max and have practiced it.

Dose & timingFor 70 kg body mass

ProtocolDoseFor youWhen
Conservative 0.2 g/kg 14 g · ~2¾ tsp Split: half 180 min pre-start, half 90 min pre-start, both with a carb-rich meal.
Standard 0.3 g/kg 21 g · ~4 tsp Split: 3–4 portions every 30–40 min, finishing 60–90 min before start, each with food.

1 level teaspoon of baking soda ≈ 5–6 g. Dissolve in ~250 ml water; chase each dose with another ~250 ml plain water and food (a bagel, banana, oats).

GI risk

GI risk is real. Common: bloating, nausea, diarrhea, vomiting. Mitigate by (1) split-dosing, (2) taking with carb-rich food, (3) drinking plenty of water, (4) starting at 0.2 g/kg the first 2–3 times. Never race-day-debut bicarb. Practice it 3–5 times on long runs first.

Skip if

Skip bicarb if you have high blood pressure, kidney issues, or a history of bicarb-induced GI distress that didn't resolve with practice. The extra sodium (~1100 mg per 3 g of baking soda) stacks on top of your in-race sodium plan — don't double up.

How

How to take it. Mix the dose into water (it fizzes and tastes salty; lemon juice helps). Drink with your pre-race meal. Buffering peaks 90–180 min after the last dose, which is why split-loading 60–180 min pre-start works.

Context

Marathon context. Bicarb's evidence base is strongest for 1–10 min all-out efforts; for the marathon, expect a smaller and more individual benefit. If your goal pace is conversational, the GI risk likely outweighs the gain — skip it.
